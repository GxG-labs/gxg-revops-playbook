# Case Formats Reference

## External Patterns

### Meta-Style Success Story

Observed pattern:

- Outcome-led headline.
- Key metrics near the top.
- Short company context.
- Challenge.
- Solution.
- Results.
- Optional executive quote.

Use when the case is for a deck, website, sales proof, or portfolio.

Best for:

- Marketing cases.
- Product growth cases.
- AI implementation cases.
- Automation cases with clear metrics.

Avoid:

- Long background.
- Tool lists without business logic.
- Results that do not connect to the solution.

### MBA-Style Business Case

Observed pattern:

- Real business situation.
- Protagonist or decision maker.
- Incomplete information and constraints.
- Decision point or tension.
- Evidence, alternatives, tradeoffs.
- Quantitative and qualitative analysis.
- Discussion-worthy lesson.

Use when the case needs credibility, strategic depth, or executive weight.

Best for:

- Transformation projects.
- Complex growth or operations cases.
- Cases with multiple stakeholders.
- Cases where the main value is judgment, not only execution.

Avoid:

- Presenting the answer too early in the full version.
- Turning every case into a linear success story if the real value was diagnosis, choice, and tradeoff management.

### B2B Sales Case Logic

Strong B2B case libraries from companies such as Gong, Outreach, HubSpot, Salesforce, Gartner, Challenger, and Forrester-style ROI studies tend to share these patterns:

- Lead with business outcomes: win rate, pipeline, revenue per rep, forecast accuracy, ramp time, ROI, cost savings.
- Show the operating problem behind the metric: weak adoption, poor visibility, inconsistent messaging, low-quality activity, scattered data, slow ramp, pipeline leakage.
- Make the buyer's internal case easier: who cares, why now, what risk was removed, what economic value changed.
- Include the mechanism of change: coaching, segmentation, workflow, automation, CRM hygiene, conversation intelligence, buyer enablement, data unification.
- Mention adoption, not just deployment: what reps, managers, marketers, operators, or leaders started doing differently.
- Use commercial insight: reframe the buyer's problem in a sharper way than they would describe it themselves.

Use B2B logic by default. Even non-sales cases should make the economic buyer clear and connect the result to revenue, cost, speed, quality, risk, or scale.

## Case Pyramid Templates

### One-Line Essence

```text
Built [system/change] for [team/company] to solve [business constraint], resulting in [main measurable outcome].
```

Alternative:

```text
Helped [team/company] move from [broken state] to [better state] through [solution], improving [business metric].
```

### One-Paragraph Case

```text
[Company/team] needed to [business goal] while [constraint]. The existing process was [problem], which created [business cost/risk]. I/we built [solution] that [core mechanism] and connected it to [systems/processes/team adoption]. The key decision was [strategic choice], because [reason]. As a result, [metric/result]. The case shows how [reusable lesson].
```

### One-Slide Case

```text
[TITLE]

[FUNCTION / BUSINESS MOTION]


BUSINESS PROBLEM

[Team/company] needed to [revenue or efficiency goal], but [constraint].
The real issue was [deeper bottleneck], causing [pipeline, conversion,
speed, forecast, quality, cost, or risk impact].


WHAT CHANGED

Built [system/process/workflow] that helped [roles] do [new behavior].
The solution connected [data/process/tools] and made [insight/action]
repeatable across the team.


BUSINESS IMPACT

[Metric/result 1]
[Metric/result 2]
[Metric/result 3]
```

### Full Web-Page Case

```text
# [Outcome-led headline]

[Lead paragraph: who, what changed, main result, why it mattered.]

## At a Glance

- Client / context: [name or anonymized sector]
- Function: [sales, marketing, operations, recruiting, support]
- Challenge: [one line]
- Solution: [one line]
- Result: [one to three metrics]
- Timeframe: [if known]

## The Challenge

[Describe the business situation. Include team size, volume, speed, quality,
cost, revenue, risk, or adoption pressure if confirmed.]

## The Decision

[Explain what had to be chosen. Include tradeoffs: speed vs quality, automation
vs control, cost vs reliability, scale vs brand consistency.]

## The Solution

[Explain what was built or changed. Name tools only when they help credibility.
Focus on workflow, data flow, integration, human review, quality gates, and
ownership.]

## Implementation

[Describe rollout. Include pilot, iteration, integration, training, acceptance
criteria, and operating rhythm if known.]

## Results

[Put metrics first. Explain what each metric means in business terms. If only
qualitative results are known, state them clearly without pretending they are
measured.]

## Why It Worked

[Summarize the operating principle. Make this useful for another executive.]
```

## Business Angles

Choose one primary angle:

- Revenue growth: conversion, reply rate, bookings, pipeline, retention.
- Cost reduction: labor hours, cost per task, overhead, media waste.
- Speed: launch time, response time, cycle time, decision time.
- Quality: consistency, brand fit, fewer errors, better decisions.
- Scale: more volume with same team.
- Risk control: compliance, approvals, human-in-the-loop, audit trail.
- Knowledge leverage: onboarding, reusable memory, internal source of truth.

## B2B Sales Motions

For B2B cases, identify the primary motion:

- New pipeline creation: outreach, ABM, lead qualification, meetings, opportunities.
- Pipeline conversion: win rate, close rate, sales cycle, deal quality, buyer engagement.
- Sales productivity: revenue per rep, activity quality, manager coaching, automation, admin reduction.
- Forecast and RevOps: forecast accuracy, pipeline hygiene, deal risk, next steps, CRM data quality.
- Ramp and enablement: onboarding time, quota attainment, playbooks, messaging consistency.
- Expansion and retention: renewals, upsell, customer health, NRR, CSAT.
- Data and AI foundation: unified customer data, next-best action, prioritization, buyer context.

## B2B Metrics to Prefer

Use these before vanity metrics:

- Pipeline created
- Qualified opportunities
- Win rate / close rate
- Sales cycle length
- Average contract value / average deal size
- Revenue per rep
- Quota attainment
- Forecast accuracy
- Ramp time to quota
- Reply rate and meeting rate
- Conversion by stage
- Pipeline coverage
- Renewal rate / net revenue retention
- CAC payback, ROI, cost savings

Use activity metrics only when they connect to outcomes. Explain how more calls, emails, demos, content, or CRM updates changed quality, conversion, speed, or cost.

## Buyer Enablement

A B2B case should help the reader answer internal questions:

- Why change now?
- What breaks if we keep the current process?
- Which team benefits first?
- What will sales, marketing, customer success, finance, operations, or leadership do differently?
- How fast can value appear?
- What risks are controlled?
- What proof would a CFO, CRO, CMO, COO, or CEO trust?

Map value to roles when relevant:

- CEO: strategic growth, operating leverage, speed.
- CFO: ROI, payback, cost reduction, headcount leverage.
- CRO: revenue predictability, win rate, pipeline quality.
- VP Sales: rep productivity, coaching, quota attainment.
- RevOps: data quality, process visibility, forecast accuracy.
- CMO / Demand Gen: pipeline source quality, messaging, conversion.
- Customer Success: renewals, expansion, customer health.

## Commercial Insight Pattern

Use this when the case needs more executive weight:

```text
The issue was not [surface problem]. The real constraint was [deeper business problem].
Once the team could [new capability], they could [business outcome].
```

Examples:

```text
The issue was not low rep activity. The real constraint was inconsistent call quality and weak pipeline prioritization.
```

```text
The issue was not a lack of content. The real constraint was that sellers could not match the right message to the right buyer moment.
```

## Result Language

Use strong result phrasing:

- `increased conversion by X`
- `reduced cost by X`
- `cut launch time from X to Y`
- `scaled output X-fold without increasing team size`
- `processed X items per month`
- `reduced first response time to X`
- `preserved brand consistency across X formats`

Use cautious result phrasing when causality is weaker:

- `supported`
- `contributed to`
- `helped enable`
- `created the operating system for`
- `gave the team visibility into`

## Red Flags

Fix these before final output:

- The case starts with a tool name instead of a business problem.
- Challenge is vague: "needed to improve efficiency."
- Solution is a list of tools.
- Results are generic: "better quality" or "more efficient."
- Metrics lack baseline or business meaning.
- The case claims strategic impact but only describes production work.
- The slide has too many words.
- The web version has no decision, tradeoff, or proof.
- The case talks about AI or software, but not buyer behavior, team behavior, or business workflow.
- Metrics are activity-only and do not map to pipeline, revenue, efficiency, risk, retention, or cost.
- No adoption story.
- No before-state.
- No proof of quality, only volume.
